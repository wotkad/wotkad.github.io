How it started…

The Price Cap Rules were implemented initially by G7 countries, namely:

  • • the United States,
  • • the United Kingdom,
  • • Canada,
  • • France,
  • • Germany,
  • • Italy,
  • • and Japan.

With the European Union (EU), Australia, Switzerland and the European Economic Area (EEA), following suit.

This collective is now referred to as G7+ countries.

All persons from these aforementioned jurisdictions combined are prohibited from partaking in any activities or aiding any transactions with Russian oil or petroleum products - Unless they are sold at, or below the specified Price Cap.

Whilst the legalities are very similar across all G7+ countries, there will be some differences, so therefore it’s going to instil greater confidence to confirm the exacting rules by country, rather than expecting the laws to automatically be the same.

However, whilst it is important to understand the legislation of each country, our main focus will generally be on the US, the EU and the UK, as these jurisdictions are mostly applicable in the interest of our stakeholders in the international oil trade and maritime shipping business, and therefore is our greatest challenge.

Detailed information and rules has been published in the ‘Guidelines’ by each jurisdiction and are available here by the links. The guidelines have been made clear, transparent and explicit by the countries that expect the Price Cap Rules to be applied to all transactions and the required authentications to showcase 100% compliancy.

This summary of the Guidelines is also accessible in the LITASCO Compliance Operational Guidance in order for all operations to be completed in accordance with the relevant laws.

The following are mandatory considerations:

  • • Every trade deal
  • • All import/export operations
  • • All services and transactions
  • • All necessary approvals and notifications (including approvals from local or other State authorities, if required).

It is this level of detail of compliance that is imperative for the continuation of not only our international trade, but also of our continued successes in the future, all of which we want you to be part of.

The Price Cap Rules ONLY apply to individuals and companies that fall under the laws of G7+ countries.

However, it is not quite that straightforward as the definition of exactly who the jurisdiction applies to is dependent on each specific country.

The following is a breakdown of who has to apply the Price Cap Rules by country:

  • • US - US citizens wherever located, or U.S. green card holders, wherever located, or anybody physically located in the U.S., or any person who is paid by a U.S. entity, or US based companies or an overseas branch.

  • • EU - citizens of EU member states wherever located or anyone physically located in the EU, or anyone paid by an EU entity or its overseas branch, or EU based companies or an overseas branch.

  • • UK - UK citizens wherever located or anyone physically located in the UK, or anyone paid by an UK entity or its overseas branch, or UK based companies or an overseas branch.

  • • Australia - citizens of the Commonwealth of Australia wherever located or anyone physically located in Australia, or anyone paid by an Australian entity or its overseas branch, or AU based companies or an overseas branch.

  • • Canada - citizens of Canada wherever located or anyone physically located in Canada, or anyone paid by a Canadian entity or its overseas branch, or Canadian based companies or an overseas branch.

  • • EEA - citizens of European Economic Association (Iceland, Lichtenstein, and Norway) member states wherever located or anyone physically located in the EEA member state, or anyone paid by an EEA entity or it’s overseas branch, or EEA based companies or its overseas branches.

  • • Swiss citizens who reside and are employed overseas are exempt from the jurisdiction.

Whilst we must ensure that we are fully compliant with the laws of G7+ countries as applicable, it is also important to note that all individuals and companies that do not fall under the jurisdiction of any G7+ countries do not have to comply with the Price Cap Rules and are free to trade and to brokers services as they have always done.

Trade deals and transactions, with individuals and companies not under G7+ jurisdiction are not required to comply with Price Cap Rules.

However, if any G7+ persons are involved in any aspect of our business, the Price Cap Rules must be applied.

This includes:

Trading and/or Maritime transportation (or associated services),including brokering, financing, financial assistance, shipping, insurance, flagging and technical assistance (does not include vessel or cargo testing, inspection or certification services).

It is important to once again note that any persons or organisations that fall under G7+ legislation are unable to provide maritime transportation (or associated services) that aids the movement of Russian oil or any oil related products from Russia to a third country unless the price is above the Cap.

So now that we have established what the rules are, we now need to gain an understanding of how it is computed.

The current caps stand as follows:

Crude Oil is US$60 per barrel.

Products traded at a Premium to Crude is US$100 per barrel. It can be noted that Premium to Crude products include gasoline, motor spirits, aviation spirits, motor fuel blend stocks, gasohol, diesel fuel, kerosene, kerosene-type jet fuel, and vacuum gas oil).

Products traded at a discount to crude is US$45 per barrel. Discount to crude products include, residual fuel oils, naphtha, and waste oils.

And once again, this is how the figures are calculated as of today, but remain subject to change at the discretion of member of G7+ coalition. If changes to the price caps do occur, we believe that there will be a reasonable notice period to allow us time to make the necessary changes to adhere to the new rules.

For more information or to confirm the current price caps, please refer to the resources available on the intranet.

It is important to distinguish exactly what is included in the Price Cap.

As of this moment the unit price of oil per barrel (CN 2709) and the unit price of oil products per barrel (CN 2710) are being capped by G7+ countries.

Other costs such as shipping, freight, customs and insurance are currently not subject to the price cap rules and remain at the FOB (free on board) price.

This is where it becomes vital that the distinction is made.

Trading contracts must be transparent, clearly showcasing the price per barrel at the ‘capped’ rates and the ‘other’ or ancillary costs must be listed and invoiced separately from the unit prices.

This is to ensure that we are offering services at fair market value and not increasing the cost to offset the losses being incurred due to the Price Cap Rules.

When does the Price Cap start?

This is a very important question, that has a surprisingly simple answer.

‘The Price Cap Rules are applicable from the exact moment that the cargo is loaded on board a vessel’. And this is the case for all transactions.

The price cap applies to all transactions until such time:

  • • When the cargo has cleared customs and landed at its destination (outside of Russia).

  • • When Russian crude oil (CN/HS 2709) is transformed into a product that has a different code.

  • • Or when Russian petroleum products (CN/HS 2710) undergo substantial processing through a blending operation in a third country with non-Russian origin product(s) and results in a change to the output coding.

However, this is a slightly grey area which is under the attention of regulators and of the industry, because if the cargo is re-exported out by sea again, without significant transformation, the Price Cap Rules need to be reapplied, irregardless of completing the initial shipment. And the same notion is applied if after landing in a third country, the oil is re-loaded onto a vessel without having undergone an economically justified substantial transformation, it is again subject to the price cap.

Yet, despite these situations where the price cap stops being applicable, it is imperative to note that even if CN/HS 2709 or 2710 has been subject to substantial transformation or blending process, it is still prohibited to sell, transport, import or supply the resulting product(s) into any country (убираем из записи territory) that has made illegal the sale, transport, import or supply of Russian oil products in the first instance, namely G7+ countries, e.g. prohibition to bring Russian oil into the UK, US or into the EU.

Let’s talk enforcement.

We have already established that compliance regarding Price Cap Rules is mandatory but what we haven’t considered yet, is how it is actually being enforced within G7+ countries.

Price Cap Rules established a Three-Tiered system to classify actors involved in maritime transportation or associated services dependent on the nature of their involvement within the transaction and their knowledge of the price of oil.

Actors involved in trading or maritime transportation, or associated services to facilitate the movement of Russian oil or products from Russia to any third country, if they have status of G7+ persons have different obligations on retention of price information, retention and its provision to other actors, and reporting obligations as applicable.

For ease of understanding, Tier 1 actors have full knowledge of the price of oil, as well as direct involvement in Russian oil or oil related products, and Tier 3 actors have no direct access to pricing information.

But let’s look at the three tiers in detail, so we can have a better understanding.

Tier 1 actors are the parties with direct access to price information.

Examples of Tier 1 individuals are:
Commodity brokers
Oil Traders
and Importers

Tier 1 actors are required to retain all price information, including:
Invoices
Contracts
Receipts/proof of payments

This documentation can be shared with both Tier 2 and 3 organisations on request, when it is relevant.

Again, for your use the approved wording for LITASCO attestations is available in the Compliance Operational Guidance. Use of the approved wording will not only make the work easier, it should be employed for consistency across our company.

In addition, there are a number of requirements imposed by the Price Cap Rules and they are as follows:

  • • Russian Oil and oil products must be sold at, or below the Price Cap and this must be documented.
  • • This documentation must be retained for a minimum of 5 years after the seaborne transportation has occurred.
  • • There needs to be transparent sharing of information when requested, that clearly evidences compliance of Price Cap Rules.
  • • The UK has slightly different rules than it’s G7+ counterparts, in that all UK Tier 1 actors must report directly to the Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI), which is a part of HM Treasury to confirm that all sales have been made at, or below the Price Cap.

Now we need to explore the Russian Federation response to the implementation of the Price Cap Rules imposed by the G7+ countries.

Russian Federation responded by issuing a ban on the supply of Russian oil and petroleum products to any foreign party, where the contracts contain instructions to amend the marginal price in line with the G7+ Price Cap Rules.

This ban can only be overturned by special request and only at the discretion of the President of Russia.

As per Russian Presidential Decree No. 317 on the 28th April 28 2023 (amending Decree No. 961), states that; supplies of Russian oil pursuant to the international agreements of the Russian Federation entered before the effective date of the Decree, can only be honoured where the final destination is a ‘friendly’ state, and does not feature on the Russian government approved ‘unfriendly’ list.

So, as a direct result of the changing laws in Russia, Russian exporters/suppliers are obligated to do the following:

  • • ensure that there are no mechanisms for fixing the marginal price (whilst still taking into account sanctions in contracts)
  • • ensure monitoring of the non-application of the mechanism for fixing the marginal price to the final buyer
  • • send information to the customs authorities and the Ministry of Energy about non-compliance and take measures to eliminate any violations from occurring.

When the cargo arrives and clears customs at its destination outside of Russia, consigned to a landed

CONTROL OF COMPLIANCE AND ENFORCEMENT
TIER 1: Actors having direct access to Price Information in the ordinary course of business

• Service Providers:

  • Importers;
  • Commodities brokers;
  • Commodities traders.

• This includes LITASCO operating companies

• Obligations:

  • To keep the record on the price (for 5 years), being it understood that the purchase contract must set the reference price per barrel to ensure the purchase takes place at or below the cap.
  • To share the Price Information within 5 working days with Tier 2 and 3 counterparties upon request and/ or provide a signed attestation.
  • Attestations shall be prepared based on approved LITASCO wording.
  • For UK Tier 2 actors seeking attestation directly from a Tier 1 actor: to ask for and receive confirmation that their UK Tier 1 counterparty has reported its use of General Licence to HM Treasury. If they interact with a non-UK Tier 1 actor, the Tier 2 actor must itself report the situation to HM Treasury.
  • To undertake appropriate due diligence on Tier 1 providers (or any associated counterparties) to satisfy themselves, based on the information available, of the reliability and accuracy of any information provided.

• Examples of Information:

  • Invoices, contracts, receipts/ proof of payment, otherwise price cap attestation.

Retention policy 5 years beyond the end of the calendar year in which the seaborn transport takes place.

CONTROL OF COMPLIANCE AND ENFORCEMENT
TIER 2: Actors directly interacting with parties with Price Information, who are sometimes able to request and receive Price Information from their customers in the ordinary course of business

• Service Providers:

  • Financial institutions providing trade finance;
  • Shipping companies chartering a vessel;
  • Ship agents;
  • Custom brokers.

• This includes EIGER

• Obligations:

  • Before completing the transaction or providing services, to request and be in receipt of, when practicable documents showing that the oil was purchased at or below the cap, otherwise a signed attestation from their counterparty. To withdraw from the transaction or not provide services if a Tier 1 actor does not comply within
    5 working days of the request.
  • To share this Price Information/ attestation onward in the chain with any counterparty that requests it.
  • Attestations shall be obtained based on approved LITASCO wording
  • For UK Tier 2 actors seeking attestation directly from a Tier 1 actor: to ask for and receive confirmation that their UK Tier 1 counterparty has reported its use of General Licence to HM Treasury. If they interact with a non-UK Tier 1 actor, the Tier 2 actor must itself report the situation to HM Treasury.
  • To undertake appropriate due diligence on Tier 1 providers (or any associated counterparties) to satisfy themselves, based on the information available, of the reliability and accuracy of any information provided.

• Examples of Information:

  • Invoices, contracts, receipts/ proof of payment, otherwise price cap attestation.

Retention policy 5 years beyond the end of the calendar year in which the seaborn transport takes place.

CONTROL OF COMPLIANCE AND ENFORCEMENT
TIER 3: Actors with no direct access to Price Information in the ordinary course of business, who does not know and cannot access the Unit Price of the Russian oil to be (or being) supplied or delivered

• Service Providers:

  • Ship owners;
  • Ship management companies;
  • Cargo insurers;
  • P&I clubs;
  • Re-insurers;
  • Insurance brokers;
  • Flagging registries.

• Obligations:

  • Before completing the transaction or providing services, to obtain and retain customer attestations in which the customer commits to not purchase seaborne Russian oil above the price cap, for example as part of their annual insurance policy or ordinary business operations.
  • For UK Tier 3 actors seeking attestation directly from a Tier 1 actor: to ask for and receive confirmation that the UK Tier 1 counterparty has reported its use of General Licence to HM Treasury. If they interact with a non-UK Tier 1 actor, the Tier 3 actor must itself report the situation to HM Treasury.
  • To undertake appropriate due diligence on providers (or any associated counterparties) to satisfy themselves, based on the information available, of the reliability and accuracy of any information provided.

• Examples of Information:

  • Sanctions exclusion clause within policy, clause within policy that excludes coverage for activities related to the maritime transport of Russian oil purchased above the price cap, price cap attestation

Retention policy 5 years beyond the end of the calendar year in which the seaborn transport takes place.

POSSIBLE IMPLICATIONS OF NON-COMPLIANCE
  • The individual person could be liable to a maximum penalty of at least five years in prison.
  • Companies could be liable to penalties of no less than 5% of the total worldwide turnover of the legal person (company) in the business year
  • Warning Letter for less serious cases
  • Publication of details of breaching with names of persons involved
  • Civil Monetary Penalties for serious breaches
  • Criminal Liability in serious cases
  • Criminal offenders face monetary fines ranging from a few thousand dollars to several million and even imprisonment up to 30 years.

RUSSIAN SANCTIONS
COUNTER-SANCTIONS ON OIL PRICE CAP RULES
  • RU President Decree No. 961 dated December 27, 2022
  • RU Government Decree No. 118 dated January 28, 2023
  • A ban for supply of Russian oil and petroleum products to foreign persons
    if contracts contain a mechanism for fixing the marginal price in line
    with the Oil Price Cap rules
  • Supplies of Oil or Petroleum Products that fall under this ban can be made
    under the special decision of the President of Russia.
  • The effect of this Decree does not apply to the supply of Russian oil carried
    out in accordance with contracts concluded in pursuance of the international
    agreements of the Russian Federation in the field of oil supplies with states
    not included in the list of foreign states and territories approved by the
    Government of the Russian Federation that commit against the Russian
    Federation, Russian legal entities and unfriendly actions of individuals.

    • Exporters/suppliers are obliged to:

    ensure that there are no mechanisms for fixing the marginal price, taking into
    account sanctions in contracts,

    ensure monitoring of the non-application of the mechanism for fixing
    the marginal price to the final buyer,

    send information to the customs authorities and the Ministry of Energy about
    non-compliance and taking measures to eliminate violations.
